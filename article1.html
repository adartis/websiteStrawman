The Illusion of Motion: Rethinking Activity, Priorities, and Progress

In a world that prizes hustle, agility, and rapid responsiveness, it is easy to conflate motion with momentum. But as many experienced managers and policy leaders know, activity does not always equate to progress. In fact, under certain conditions, increased activity, particularly when spread across too many fronts, can diminish real-world impact.

A useful metaphor for understanding this phenomenon comes from physics: the Euler’s Disk. This fascinating object spins with increasing speed and noise just before it stops completely. At a glance, it appears to be accelerating; yet it is actually losing energy and preparing to collapse. The analogy is a compelling one for management and organisational strategy.

Displacement versus Activity: A Hypothesis

Drawing on this metaphor, we can formulate a working hypothesis:

The more frequently one acts, and the more priorities one juggles, the less displacement (that is, real forward movement) is achieved.

This suggests a kind of inverse relationship between:

  • The frequency of action (task switching, reactive activity, overcommunication),
  • The number of priorities (parallel projects, competing goals), and
  • The actual displacement: meaningful change, measurable results, or strategic advancement.

Put simply, the busier you are, the less you may be achieving.

Why This Matters for Management Theory

This is not a case against work, responsiveness, or ambition. Rather, it is a critique of undisciplined activity. In high-functioning organisations, particularly in policy, consulting, and public reform, there is a temptation to multiply effort across every possible front. But without strategic focus, this can become noise without signal.

  • Context switching consumes mental bandwidth and reduces decision quality.
  • Overlapping priorities dilute resources and weaken team coherence.
  • Frequent tactical adjustments undermine the stability needed for long-term planning.

Instead of achieving more, organisations often achieve less, but louder.

Accelerated Programmes Deserve Slower Thinking

It may seem paradoxical, but the most ambitious programmes, those with accelerated timelines or complex delivery requirements, demand fewer distractions and more sustained attention. These are the efforts that require:

  • Longer thinking cycles
  • Tighter resource alignment
  • Clearer communication boundaries

When everything is urgent, nothing is truly important. When every initiative gets equal attention, none are sufficiently nourished to succeed.

A Proposed Model

We might express this idea more formally:

Displacement ∝ 1 / (Activity Frequency × Number of Priorities)

Where:

  • Displacement = Real-world movement or impact
  • Activity Frequency = How often actions or shifts occur
  • Number of Priorities = Competing active efforts

The model warns that even highly motivated teams can spin out if stretched across too many initiatives with excessive responsiveness. The visible motion, emails, meetings, pivots, masks the underlying stall in progress.

What This Does Not Mean

This is not an argument to slow everything down or stop work altogether. Organisations should remain responsive, energetic, and goal-driven. But they must be discerning.

High-priority, accelerated programmes should not be treated like everything else. They warrant protected attention, slowed decision cycles, and greater managerial patience. In fact, the more urgent the work, the more it needs space and focus to succeed.

Conclusion: Less Frequency, More Force

Effective management is not about doing everything. It is about knowing what to focus on, what to delay, and what to drop. In today’s fast-moving environment, it is tempting to fill the calendar and tick off tasks. But smart managers understand that real change requires sustained effort, not scattered energy.

Let us stop measuring progress by how fast we spin, and start asking how far we are really moving.
