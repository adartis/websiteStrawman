The Illusion of Activity: Rethinking Pace, Priorities, and Progress

In a world that prizes hustle, agility, and rapid responsiveness, it is easy to conflate motion with momentum.

But as many experienced managers and policy leaders know, activity does not always equate to progress; under certain conditions, rapid activity can diminish real-world impact.

A useful metaphor comes from physics: the Euler’s Disk. This fascinating object spins with increasing speed and noise just before stopping completely. At a glance, it appears to accelerate—yet it is actually losing energy and preparing to collapse.

Displacement versus Activity

The more frequently one acts, and the more priorities one juggles, the less displacement (that is, real forward movement) is achieved.

There is a connection between:

  • The frequency of action (task switching, reactive activity, overcommunication)
  • The number of priorities (parallel projects, competing goals)
  • The size of the change you want to see in the world

Put simply: the busier you are, the less you may be achieving.

Why This Matters for Management

This is not a case against work, responsiveness, or ambition. It is a critique of undisciplined activity.

In high-functioning organisations – particularly in government, perhaps the most complex industry for managing expectations – there is a tendency to try to do more with less.

Without strategic focus, this becomes noise without signal.

Instead of doing more, organisations should reconsider how resources are deployed, and focus on a handful of objectives that will improve the overall system of delivery.

Most Important Programmes Deserve Slower Thinking

It may seem paradoxical, but the most ambitious programmes—those with accelerated timelines or complex delivery requirements—demand fewer distractions and more sustained attention.

These efforts require:

  • Longer thinking cycles
  • Tighter resource alignment
  • Clearer communication boundaries

When everything is urgent, nothing is truly important. When every initiative gets equal attention, none are sufficiently nourished to succeed.

What This Does Not Mean

This is not an argument to slow everything down or stop work altogether. Organisations should remain responsive, energetic, and goal-driven. But they must be discerning.

High-priority, accelerated programmes should not be treated like everything else. They warrant protected attention, slower decision cycles, and greater managerial patience.

In fact, the more urgent the work, the more it needs space and focus to succeed.

Conclusion: Less Frequency, More Force

Effective management is not about doing everything. It is about knowing what to focus on, what to delay, and what to drop.

In today’s fast-moving environment, it is tempting to fill the calendar and tick off tasks. But real change requires sustained effort, not scattered energy.
